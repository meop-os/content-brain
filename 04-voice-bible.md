# Meop Outreach Voice Bible

Every DM, email, and outreach message gets checked against this document. No exceptions.

---

## The One Rule

**Sound like a human texting a smart friend. Not a company pitching a prospect.**

Read your draft out loud. If it sounds like something a person would actually say in a bar, keep it. If it sounds like something a LinkedIn bot would send, kill it.

---

## Voice Rules (Non-Negotiable)

### 1. Fifth Grade Reading Level
Short words. Short sentences. No jargon. Lavender data shows 3rd-5th grade reading level gets 36-53% more replies than college-level writing. The brain processes simple text faster. When someone's scanning 200 emails, easy = reply.

### 2. Conversational, Not Professional
Write like you text. Not like you're writing a cover letter.

| Kill This | Write This |
|-----------|-----------|
| "I hope this email finds you well" | Just start talking |
| "I wanted to reach out because..." | Say why. No preamble. |
| "Please let me know if you have any questions" | Ask one question |
| "I'd love to schedule a call at your convenience" | "worth 15 min?" |
| "Per our conversation" | never say this |
| "Circling back" | never say this |
| "Re:" | never say this |
| "Leverage" / "synergy" / "optimize" | use normal words |

### 3. Short
- DMs: 2-4 sentences max
- Emails: 50-125 words. If you scroll once, it's too long.
- Subject lines: 2-3 words. Every word over 3 costs reply rate.
- One idea per message. Not three. One.

### 4. Lead With Their Problem
Never open with who you are or what you do. Open with something about them. Their pain, their world, their situation.

Bad: "Hi, I'm Griffin from Meop. We build AI-powered company operating systems."
Good: "saw your post about managing 12 client accounts manually. that's exactly the gap we fill."

### 5. Deliberate Human Imperfections
Perfect emails signal automation. One small human touch per message:

**Allowed imperfections:**
- Lowercase "i" at start of a casual DM
- Starting a sentence with "and" or "but"
- An ellipsis mid-thought ("we've been seeing this a lot lately... especially with teams your size")
- Slight informality ("tbh" or "ngl" if it fits the person)
- A self-correction ("well, actually let me put it differently")
- No period at end of last sentence in a DM

**Not allowed:**
- Actual typos in names, companies, or titles (kills credibility)
- Multiple errors (looks unprofessional, not human)
- Broken grammar that confuses meaning

One imperfection = human signal. Two = sloppy. This is a scalpel, not a sledgehammer.

### 6. No Em Dashes
Em dashes (---) read as AI-generated. Use periods, commas, or start a new sentence. No exceptions. This is a hard rule across all Meop outreach.

### 7. One Ask Per Message
Never qualify, pitch, AND book in the same message. Pick one micro-goal:
- Get a reply
- Get them curious
- Get 15 minutes

Not all three at once.

### 8. The Tiny Ask
Make it easy to say yes. "15 minutes" not "a demo." Use no-oriented framing from Chris Voss: "would it be crazy to compare notes?" People feel safer saying no, which lowers their guard.

## The Outreach Stack (Use This Every Time)

### Step 1: Research (5 min)
Find one of these: a trigger event, a business problem, a personal connection, a social proof bridge, or a competitive insight. If you can't find any of these in 5 minutes, the lead isn't warm enough.

### Step 2: Open With Them (Sam McKenna's "Show Me You Know Me")
One sentence. One specific observation about them. Tied to their business, not generic flattery. This alone gets 43% open rates vs 6% industry average.

### Step 3: Label the Pain (Voss + Braun)
Name their problem using their own words. Be specific. "I imagine with a team your size, [specific problem] is eating more hours than it should." Don't pitch. Provoke thinking.

### Step 4: Create Curiosity (Braun)
Open a loop without closing it. "we've seen 3 patterns with teams like yours that most people miss. happy to share if relevant." The brain needs to close incomplete patterns.

### Step 5: One Question
End on one question. Not two. Not a list of options. One question that needs a yes or no.

## Warm Outreach Sequence (LinkedIn)

Warm outreach gets 3-7x the reply rate of cold. Always warm first.

**Day 1:** Comment on their post. Thoughtful, specific. Not "great post."
**Day 3:** Connect request with 1-sentence note referencing the post.
**Day 5 (after accept):** Short DM. "appreciate the connect. your take on [topic] stuck with me. curious, is [related problem] something your team deals with?"
**Day 8:** Share one piece of value. No ask. A relevant data point, article, or insight.
**Day 12:** Soft ask. "happy to share what we've seen work for similar companies if useful. no pressure either way."

### What Kills LinkedIn Outreach
- Connection request + immediate pitch (pitch-slap)
- Templates that are obviously copy-pasted
- Formal language
- Multiple questions in one message
- InMail without prior engagement

## Cold Email Rules

### Subject Lines (Lavender data)
- 2-3 words optimal. 4+ words = 17.5% fewer replies.
- Use their company name (+22% opens)
- Use numbers (+113% opens)
- Don't use their first name (signals automation)
- Don't ask a question
- Use possessives ("your team," "your pipeline")
- Neutral tone. No hype words.

**Good:** "your Q3 pipeline" / "idea for [Company]" / "[Mutual] suggested this"
**Bad:** "Quick question" / "Can we chat?" / "Exciting opportunity"

### Email Body
- 50-125 words
- 1 idea per line
- Heavy whitespace
- 3rd-5th grade reading level
- Their words, not yours
- One CTA. Tiny.

### Follow-Up
- 4-8 touches total. Most replies come after touch 4.
- Space 3-7 days apart
- Vary the channel (email, LinkedIn, comment)
- Each follow-up adds new value, not "just checking in"

## Hormozi's ACA Framework (For Warm DMs)

1. **Acknowledge** something specific about them. "I saw your post on [topic]."
2. **Compliment** tied to the acknowledgment. "you clearly think about X differently than most."
3. **Ask** a transitional question. NOT a pitch. Ask for their opinion or a referral.

You are not selling. You are starting a conversation.

## The Psychology (Why People Reply)

| Trigger | How to Use It |
|---------|---------------|
| **Reciprocity** | Give something first. An insight, a resource, a specific observation. People return value. |
| **Curiosity Gap** | Open a loop. "found 3 things [competitor] does that you don't. happy to share." Don't close it. |
| **Pattern Interrupt** | Break the scan-and-delete pattern. Unexpected observation, unusual format, strange question. |
| **Social Proof** | Mention recognizable clients, mutual connections, specific results. Lowers risk of engaging. |
| **Loss Aversion** | Frame around what they're losing, not what they could gain. "most teams your size are leaving [X] on the table." |

## Opening Lines That Work (Ranked)

1. **Trigger-based:** "saw [Company] just [hired 20 people / raised / launched X]..."
2. **Observation:** "noticed [specific thing] and had an immediate thought..."
3. **Mutual connection:** "[name] suggested I reach out. said you're dealing with [problem]..."
4. **Contrarian:** "most [personas] I talk to focus on [common approach]. I think that's backwards..."
5. **Pain hypothesis:** "I imagine with a team your size, [problem] eats more time than it should..."
6. **Compliment + bridge:** "your post on [topic] was the clearest take I've seen. made me wonder if you've thought about [related thing]..."

## Opening Lines to Kill

- "Hope you're doing well"
- "I wanted to introduce myself"
- "My name is [X] and I work at [Y]"
- "I came across your profile"
- "We help companies like yours"
- "I know you're busy, but"

## Before You Send Checklist

- [ ] Would I actually say this to someone at a bar? If no, rewrite.
- [ ] Is it under 125 words?
- [ ] Does it start with THEM, not me?
- [ ] Is there only ONE ask?
- [ ] Is the reading level 5th grade or below?
- [ ] Zero em dashes?
- [ ] Does it have one human imperfection?
- [ ] Would I reply to this if I received it?
